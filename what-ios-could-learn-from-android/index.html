Things iOS could learn from Android

By Benoît Boucart 16 May 2015

I recently moved from Android to iOS. I’m happy with my new toy but I miss some features of Google’s mobile OS.

In January I received a brand new iPhone 6 from my work. I was very excited to have a phone with an OS I never used for personal purpose. The move to iOS was not a simple task for the loyal Android-user I was.

This article is a collection of random features I wished iOS had.

Keyboard case

One of the most annoying things about iOS for me was the keyboard, and especially its upper/lowercase button.

Do you notice the difference? Can you find out which one is upper- or lowercase? I don’t understand the design decision behind this.

This one is a bit clearer, no? The iOS keyboard is really a pain in the neck. That’s why I gave SwiftKey Keyboard a chance. I used it for some weeks, but because of the choppy support of customized keyboards in iOS, I finally ditched it. And I’m still not used to this weird behavior.

The iOS clock app

Another iOS functionality that still frustrates me is the default clock & alarm app.

Why do I need to tap on the “edit” button to edit an alarm, or even worse, to enable it? Wouldn’t it be easier to tap on the alarm and just edit it?

What I also miss is the Android feature that is showing the remaining time before the alarm will go off. That gave me an extra confidence that I didn’t set a wrong time. Another benefit of this was that I knew how much time I could sleep (and possibly change it).

As can be seen in the screenshot above in the native Android app you can:

  • Change the time of the alarm by tapping on it
  • Enable / disable an alarm in one tap
  • See the remaining time before the alarm will go off

iOS notification system

The notification system of iOS is not as good as the one of Android. It’s annoying you have to remove all notification manually. You can only remove multiple notifications if you double tab on the “cross” that belong to an app.

In Android you can just remove all your notification at once and no double tap is required:

Apple store

The search in the Apple store is not as smooth as Google’s play store. The results are not always as pertinent as in the Play Store. The view in Android is also much compacter.

When I look for a messenger app, I don’t want to see an Emoji pack as search result.

  • Control over Mobile internet / wifi settings
  • Close apps
  • Notifications: when you read mail, the notification is not removed

That was it. This article focused on some negative aspects but overall, I really enjoy using iOS. However, I would not say it’s better (or worse) then Android. Both have their strengths and weaknesses.
